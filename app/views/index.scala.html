About Forest School

Forest School is a Vermont-licensed early childhood education center.
Classes are offered five days a week, from 8:30AM to 3:30PM.



The Forest School program was established in Vermont in 2015 by Annabelle Knight. In 2012-2013 while working as an early childhood educator on the west coast, Annabelle began researching and learning about the benefits of nature immersion programs for young children. She met Magdalena Cabrera, owner and head teacher of a nature awareness preschool in Northern California since 1988. After interning with Magdalena, Annabelle was inspired to own and operate a forest school program for children and families on the east coast closer to where she grew up.

Curriculum

The curriculum emerges from childrenâ€™s interests, relationships, daily routines, and play. The physical environment is an unlimited source of rhythm, patterns, and inspiration for small and large group projects and investigations that incorporate science, math, language arts, social studies, and creative arts. Childrenâ€™s daily experiences include opportunities to create, explore, and learn about similarities and differences among people while challenging stereotypes and bias with critical thinking and problem-solving. Assessment

Educators document individual progress using portfolio-based assessment. Portfolio entries include pieces of work, photos, videos, and written notes collected over time by educators and children. Throughout the process, children are encouraged to self-reflect on their learning journey and feel proud of their individual growth. Educators use their expertise in child development and examine current research to inform appropriate learning goals (i.e. scientific reasoning, mathematical thinking, language, communication, social, emotional and physical development).

The methodology of Forest School (similar to Forest Kindergartens, Rain or Shine Schools, Nature Kindergartens, and Nature Preschools) is validated by growing international research that outdoor-based education programs enrich childrenâ€™s learning and development while fostering deep connections and sustainable relationships with the natural environment.

Play

Forest School emphasizes a childâ€™s right to play in nature. Our program incorporates at least 3-4 hours of free, child-centered play outdoors each day. Although childrenâ€™s play might appear simple and repetitive, they are demonstrating tremendous creativity and mastery of complex concepts. For example, a 4-year-old pretending to fly a plane by sitting on top of a fallen tree is using her imagination to construct symbolic meaning, a complicated cognitive task. She might ask peers to join her and act as passengers which reveals her developing ability to communicate and collaborate with others. Researchers have documented significant evidence that unstructured play contributes to school readiness and is valuable for healthy brain development including social, emotional, and physical skills4,6. Ultimately, play enables children to direct their own learning and development.

Engaging with Nature

The natural world presents a huge variety of learning opportunities, health and wellness benefits. Furthermore, it is crucial for children to spend time interacting with and appreciating nature if we want to promote a sustainable planet for future generations. Experts agree that time spent outside encourages positive social, cognitive, emotional and physical development3. When it comes to the best environments for childrenâ€™s learning, the natural world promotes the ideal balance of healthy risks, challenges and fun. Children and adults experience restorative cognitive functioning, improved motor skills and lower stress levels when interacting with or looking at nature compared to non-natural, urban environments2,10,11. Additionally, some children have shown to have enhanced ability to concentrate when spending time near natural environments8.

Physical Activity

The outdoor environment is more likely to encourage active play (also referred to as MVPA or moderate to vigorous physical activity)5. Research shows that nearly half of preschool children do not get enough active play or MVPA9. Current governmental guidelines recommend that children ages 3-5 engage in at least 60 minutes of MVPA every day. We provide children with 2-3 more hours on average of outdoor play each day than typical preschools. Our program is designed with the knowledge that children are healthier in the long-term, more able to learn, and better able to self-regulate when their entire bodies are actively engaged1,7.

Commonly Asked Questions

  • Do you always go outside?
    Yes! If weather conditions drop below 15 degrees or above 90 degrees Fahrenheit, there is heavy precipitation, thunder, lightning or high winds, childrenâ€™s time outdoors will be limited. Educators follow exposure recommendations from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Association (NOAA) website for wind chill and frostbite risk. We have many options for indoor activities in our home-like schoolhouse that help children build connections with nature. For example, we incorporate clay, sticks, and pine cones during indoor play.
  • Are there requirements for this program?
    Yes. All children must have turned 3½ years of age by the time they are enrolled and attending classes. Additionally, children must be toilet-trained and properly dressed for the outdoors. Please read â€˜Required Clothing and Gearâ€™ in the Family Handbook for further information.
  • What is your teacher to child ratio?
    We aim to enroll 6-9 children during the first year, so the maximum staff to child ratio is 1:6 while the minimum is 1:4Â½.
  • What qualifications do the teachers have?
    The lead educator, Annabelle Knight, has a Masterâ€™s Degree in Early Childhood Education from Lesley University and has been teaching professionally since 2010. She is licensed by the VT Agency of Education and certified by the VT Child Development Division.
  • How can you keep children safe outside?
    While there are unique hazards associated with outdoor learning, safety is our top priority. Educators are First Aid/CPR Certified and practice daily safety routines, such as conducting daily risk assessments of the natural environment, identifying and discussing boundaries and potential hazards with the children, always carrying a cell phone, asking that all children must be within sight of an adult and using whistles and songs to alert children. During warmer months, families need to help protect children from ticks by checking their skin each day.
  • Are snacks and lunch provided?
    Yes and No. Lunch is not provided. Snacks are provided. We always offer healthy snacks in the morning and afternoon. When possible, we source local food that has been grown using organic practices. We also work with families around children's particular allergies, restrictions and sensitivities. We recommend zero-waste lunches that use reusable packaging and promote healthy eating habits. If children forget their lunch at home, the following items will be available:
    • Fresh fruits and vegetables, such as apples, pears, carrots and peppers
    • Whole grain bread, crackers, gluten-free rice cakes
    • Hummus and black bean dip
  • How far do you travel?
    When children are newly enrolled, our adventure hikes will consist of about Â½ -1Â½ miles total distance walking. As children grow stronger and more familiar with the routine, we can walk up to 3 miles in a morning.
  • What is anti-bias education and why is it important?
    Research has shown that children as young as 3-4 years old have developed biases around differences such as skin color and ability. We want to promote equity and justice by actively challenging bias and prejudice in everyday life. We emphasize feeling comfortable and proud of unique attributes and common humanity. For example, childrenâ€™s books are closely examined by educators and children for stereotypical reflections of people and families. Educators encourage open discussion of character portrayals, differences and similarities in an effort to achieve the anti-bias goals outlined below.

    • Anti-bias Education Goals

    • Each child will demonstrate self-awareness, confidence, family pride, and positive social identities.
    • Each child will express comfort and joy with human diversity; accurate language for human differences; and deep, caring human connections.
    • Each child will increasingly recognize unfairness, have language to describe unfairness, and understand that unfairness hurts.
    • Each child will demonstrate empowerment and the skills to act, with others or alone, against prejudices and/or discriminatory actions.
    Derman-Sparks & Edwards, Anti-bias Education for Young Children and Ourselves 2010, p.xiv
  • What does family participation look like?
    Families are always welcome and encouraged to visit the school. We love to have visitors help out and teach us new things whether it is for 5 minutes, 45 minutes or a day. We ask that families who are visiting, dress appropriately for the weather, including wearing similar items that we ask children to wear in order to model appropriate gear. When participating, we ask families to adhere to the following policies:
    • Adults do not help children climb higher than the child can climb by him or herself.
    • We avoid picking up items that are still alive.
    • Children may take home pieces that are smaller than a finger and inanimate.
    • We can use sticks as long as the child has enough space.
    • Smoking is prohibited on all school property.
    • Visitors will not take any pictures or videos of the children in our program.
