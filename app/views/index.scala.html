Curriculum

The curriculum emerges primarily from the natural world, children’s interests, play, and daily routines. Children’s self-directed activities, especially in the outdoors inspire play scenarios, class discussions, and projects that extend over weeks and months. Children develop skills like critical thinking, problem-solving, and effective decision-making while accomplishing meaningful tasks. Educators also guide explorations of family and community to reinforce group connections and healthy identity development.

Throughout daily activities, educators support children’s emotional, social, physical, and cognitive development. The physical environment is an unlimited source of rhythm, patterns, and inspiration for small and large group projects and investigations that incorporate science, math, language arts, social studies, and creative arts. The program includes high-quality learning methods and materials such as diverse genres of literature, storytelling, woodworking and baking activities, songs and fingerplays, developmentally appropriate technology, musical instruments, fine arts and natural materials found in nature.

Assessment

Educators document individual progress using portfolio-based assessment. Portfolio entries include pieces of work, photos, videos, and written notes collected over time by educators and children. Throughout the process, children are encouraged to self-reflect on their learning and feel proud of their individual growth. Educators use their expertise in child development and examine current research to inform appropriate learning goals (i.e. scientific reasoning, mathematical thinking, language, communication, social, emotional, and physical achievements).

For a more detailed explanation of target developmental learning goals and typical school activities, please view the Learning Objectives below.

Mission & Philosophy

Mission Statement

Forest School offers an early childhood environmental education program that nurtures children’s development and connections with nature. We support a diverse and inclusive community of children, families, and educators who respect and care for themselves, each other, and the Earth.

Forest School gives equal treatment and access to our program and to current or prospective employees without discrimination based on ancestry, color, disability, ethnic group orientation, family structure, gender, gender identity or expression, marital status, medical condition, national origin, race, religious creed, sexual orientation, or socioeconomic status.

Philosophy

Forest School incorporates child-centered, inquiry and play-based educational philosophies. Our Guiding Principles are:

  • Nature provides an ideal context for child-led learning
  • Children and families are competent, capable, and resilient
  • Diversity and inclusion are essential in all aspects of the school

Children develop respect and compassion for themselves, each other, and the environment when given opportunities to ask questions, take risks, explore and discover their world. Educators guide children’s investigations, and design and maintain indoor and outdoor spaces. In addition to providing respectful caregiving, staff mentor and partner with children to support their strengths, challenges, and unique identities. We challenge stereotypes and unfairness through inclusive conversations and stories. We value and advocate for families, their diversity, unique perspectives and expert knowledge of their children.

Play

Forest School emphasizes a child’s right to play which is essential to learning and healthy development4. Our program incorporates at least 3-4 hours of child-directed play outdoors each day. Child-directed play enables children to problem-solve, make their own decisions, collaborate with peers, and test evolving theories of how the world works2. In addition to providing time for free play, educators use guided play approaches such as preparing materials based on children’s interests and posing open-ended questions to focus and extend learning14. Research shows that unstructured and guided play, with the support of nurturing and respectful educators, are effective for developing school/life readiness (i.e. social, emotional, cognitive, and physical skills)4,9,14. Some research also demonstrates that guided play approaches are more effective for children’s learning outcomes in math and literacy than academic or free play approaches15.

Engaging with Nature

The natural world presents a variety of unique learning opportunities, health, and wellness benefits. Experts agree that time spent outside supports positive social, cognitive, emotional, and physical development2,3. Studies show that children who interact with nature have improved motor skills, language, concentration, emotional regulation, and lower stress levels7,12,16. There is also evidence that spending more time outside reduces the incidence of nearsightedness5. Furthermore, researchers have found links between the amount of time young people spend in nature, their appreciation and connection to the natural world, and their attitudes towards caring for it in the future3.

Physical Activity

Children are more likely to engage in physical activity outdoors11. Health agencies recommend that preschool children engage in at least 60-120 minutes of physical activity every day6,13. While research shows that children in preschool settings spend most of their time inactive indoors, at least 70% of our program day offers active outdoor play opportunities8,11. Our program is designed so that children are healthier in the long-term, better able to self-regulate, and more able to learn because their entire bodies are actively engaged1,10.

Commonly Asked Questions

  • Do you always go outside?
    Yes! If weather conditions drop below 15 degrees or above 90 degrees Fahrenheit, there is heavy precipitation, thunder, lightning or high winds, children’s time outdoors will be limited. Educators follow exposure recommendations from the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Association (NOAA) website for wind chill and frostbite risk. We have many options for indoor activities in our schoolhouse that help children build connections with nature. For example, educators offer natural elements such as clay, sticks, and pine cones during extended time indoors.
  • Are there requirements for this program?
    Yes. All children need to be 3½ years of age by the time they are enrolled and attending classes. Additionally, children need to be using the toilet independently and properly dressed for the outdoors. Please read ‘Required Clothing and Gear’ in the Family Handbook for further information. If you need assistance acquiring gear, please ask an educator for additional information. There are affordable second-hand stores and local resources that can help.
  • What is your teacher to child ratio?
    The maximum staff to child ratio is 1:6.
  • What qualifications do the teachers have?
    The lead educator, Annabelle Knight, has a Master’s Degree in Early Childhood Education from Lesley University and has been teaching professionally since 2010. She is licensed by the VT Agency of Education and certified by the VT Child Development Division.
  • How can you keep children safe outside?
    While there are unique hazards associated with outdoor learning, safety is our first priority. Educators carry backpacks with safety supplies and cell phones, are First Aid/CPR Certified, and have been trained in a comprehensive set of emergency preparedness plans and procedures. We conduct daily safety routines including headcounts and risk assessments of individuals, materials, and the environment. Children work with staff to identify and discuss boundaries, potential hazards, and what to do when they hear a safety whistle. While all children must stay within sight and earshot of an adult, each child is given a safety whistle and instructed how to use it in an emergency. During warmer months, families need to help protect children from ticks by checking their skin each day.
  • What is anti-bias education and why is it important?
    Research has shown that children as young as 3-4 years old have developed biases around differences such as skin color and ability. We want to challenge bias and prejudice in everyday life. We emphasize feeling comfortable and proud of unique attributes and common humanity. For example, children’s books are closely examined by educators and children for stereotypical reflections of people and families. Educators encourage open discussion of character portrayals, differences and similarities in an effort to achieve the anti-bias goals outlined below.

    • Anti-bias Education Goals

    • Each child will demonstrate self-awareness, confidence, family pride, and positive social identities.
    • Each child will express comfort and joy with human diversity; accurate language for human differences; and deep, caring human connections.
    • Each child will increasingly recognize unfairness, have language to describe unfairness, and understand that unfairness hurts.
    • Each child will demonstrate empowerment and the skills to act, with others or alone, against prejudices and/or discriminatory actions.
    Derman-Sparks & Edwards, Anti-bias Education for Young Children and Ourselves 2010, p.xiv
  • Are snacks and lunch provided?
    Snacks are provided. Lunch is not provided. Packed lunch foods must be non-perishable due to the nature of our outdoor program and state licensing regulations. We recommend zero-waste lunches that use reusable packaging and promote healthy eating habits. We offer healthy snacks in the morning and afternoon. When possible, we source local food that has been grown using organic practices. We also work with families around children's particular allergies and dietary needs. Snacks provided may include:
    • Fresh fruits and vegetables, such as apples, pears, carrots and peppers
    • Whole grain bread, crackers, gluten-free rice cakes
    • Nut and seed butter, hummus, and black bean dip
  • Will my child be prepared for elementary school and beyond?
    Yes. Time spent in nature and high-quality play have been shown to support children’s learning and development of foundational skills and complex cognitive tasks needed for academic work. Children will leave the program with a love of learning, creativity, resilience, critical thinking, and problem-solving skills to help them adapt and thrive in new environments.
